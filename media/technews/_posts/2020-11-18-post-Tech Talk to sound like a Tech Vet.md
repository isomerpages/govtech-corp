<h3>Agile vs waterfall!</h3>
<p>You may have heard the term ‚Äúagile‚Äù thrown around a bit in project management,

<p>The waterfall method is linear (everything follows a straight line) and

<p>Waterfall works well for straightforward stuff with well-established protocols

And once this masterplan is locked in place, it brooks no changes.</p>
<p>Think of it as building an eight-lane super highway to connect two land

<p>Agile, on the other hand, as its name suggests, accommodates changes well.
It sees a project as a series of incremental steps, with each iteration

As a problem-solving mechanism, the philosophy is: ‚ÄúLet‚Äôs not sit around
cooking up a perfect plan. Instead, let‚Äôs come up with a quick fix and
see how that works out while tweaking it as we go along.‚Äù</p>
<p>Going back to our analogy of connecting two land masses. Instead of planning

the traffic volume, you might realise you don‚Äôt need so many lanes after
all. Instead, there is demand for bicycle lanes and pedestrian tracks because

are already happily flowing back and forth.</p>
<h3>Scrum</h3>
<p>No, its not that rugby scrum that you are thinking off. Decidedly less
physical, scrum, when it comes to tech, is a framework to implement an
agile style of working. So a description of scrum sounds similar to agile,
but just remember: Agile is more of a philosophy, while scrum is the skeleton
that gives it structure.</p>
<p>So, when creating a product in a scrum structure, each iteration of the
product is known as a sprint, which usually lasts one or two weeks. The
short time period allows nimbleness. Need some changes or bugs to be ironed
out? No problem, we‚Äôll just work that into the next sprint, which is, at
most, two weeks away.</p>
<p>However, this flexibility can also become a liability if it leads to mission

everyone has an opinion on what should be added, and it gets bloated into
an unwieldy Frankenstein.</p>
<p>To prevent this, the team must maintain a clear vision of what is being
built and steer together towards that objective, taking on board useful
changes that add to that goal and jettisoning others that are distractions.</p>
<h3>Stand Up</h3>
<p>One key aspect of sprints is ensuring everyone is on the same page. To

<h3>Retro</h3>
<p>Retro ‚Äì short for retrospective ‚Äì in this case is a meeting at the end

<p>The team usually talks about what went well, what can be improved, and
raises any doubts they have. This is useful as good practices can be kept,
while proposed improvements can be immediately adopted at the next sprint.</p>

<p>Ever been stuck while shopping, unsure whether outfit A or B suits you
