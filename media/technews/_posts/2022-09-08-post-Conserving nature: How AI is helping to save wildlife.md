<p>Technology and nature are often thought of as polar opposites. But the
ever-expanding world of artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly being
used for conservation not just across the globe but right here in Singapore
too.</p>
<p>Still not convinced that tech can be an ally instead of an enemy of nature?
Then, let‚Äôs walk through some examples, starting from home.</p>
<h3>Fin Finder app to identify illegal shark and ray trading (Singapore)</h3>
<p>There has been growing awareness over the years of the need to protect
sharks and rays. Still, about 100 million sharks are killed each year,
many due to the demands of the shark fin trade.</p>
<p>Singapore is part of a global convention banning the trade of certain
endangered sharks and rays, but enforcement is a challenge as it is not
easy to easily tell if dried fins and rays come from the restricted species.</p>
<p>The&nbsp;<a href="https://www.nparks.gov.sg/biodiversity/centre-for-wildlife-forensics/fauna-identification-and-analysis/sharks-and-rays" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Fin Finder</a>&nbsp;app
uses AI that has been trained to identify prohibited fin types. Using the
app, inspectors armed with a simple smartphone can rapidly identify contraband.</p>

<h3>Poacher detection ‚Äì Connected Conservation Initiative (Zambia)</h3>
<p>Zambia is home to the 22,400 sq km Kafue National Park, which is supposed
to be a safe haven for wildlife. However, the vastness of the park also
makes it easy for poachers to slip in and out and evade park rangers. Illegal
fishing in the lake next to the park is also a problem.</p>
<p>The&nbsp;<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2022/feb/21/five-ways-ai-is-saving-wildlife-from-counting-chimps-to-locating-whales-aoe" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Connected Conservation Initiative</a>&nbsp;uses
infrared thermal cameras to monitor the park, and AI is deployed to automatically
detect boats entering the park‚Äôs waters so as to catch illicit fishing
activity.</p>
<p>This greatly reduces the number of rangers needed to monitor the cameras,
improving the protection of the lake.</p>
<h3>Track water loss ‚Äì MapBiomas (Brazil)</h3>
<p>Since the 1990s, Brazil has lost more than 15 per cent of its surface
water, devastating the natural habitats of countless wildlife.</p>
<p>The scale of the catastrophe came into focus thanks to MapBiomas‚Äô use
of AI, powerful enough to distinguish between natural and human-created
water bodies, to analyse over 150,000 satellite images taken from 1985
to 2020. The research showed the water changes across the country over
the decades, highlighting the severity of the situation.</p>
<p>For instance, the Pantanal ‚Äì the world‚Äôs largest tropical wetland ‚Äì has
lost 74 per cent of its surface water in the section that lies in Brazil.</p>
<h3>Finding whales around the Pacific Islands</h3>
<p>Despite their size, whales can be elusive and hard to track. The&nbsp;
<a href="https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/about-us" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Association Fisheries (NOAA)</a>&nbsp;has
been recording the singing of the world‚Äôs largest mammals in a bid to use
the distinctive acoustics to find the marine animals.</p>
<p>In 2018, NOAA teamed up with Google to create an&nbsp;<a href="https://www.blog.google/technology/ai/tale-whale-song/" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">AI model</a>&nbsp;that
can identify the songs from humpbacks. Computers analysed over 170,000
hours of underwater audio recordings, a data trove that would take a person
over 19 years to listen through even if working 24 hours a day.</p>
<p>Thanks to the breakthrough, researchers were able to identify whale activity
in an area of the ocean where their presence had not been documented before.</p>
<h3>Identify and tag locations of animals with Google‚Äôs Wildlife Insights</h3>
<p><a href="https://www.engadget.com/2019-12-17-google-wildlife-insights.html" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">Google‚Äôs Wildlife Insights</a>&nbsp;is
a searchable public&nbsp;<a href="https://www.wildlifeinsights.org" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">website</a>&nbsp;that
enables anyone to upload camera trap photos.</p>
<p>AI is used to label animals in the 39 million photos, and the repository
is organised by where the photos were taken. This gives conservationists
the ability to track animal populations in different areas and analyse
the impact of climate change and human intrusion.</p>
<h3>Collecting data on new wildlife in Singapore</h3>
<p>Despite being a city-state, Singapore is rich in biodiversity. The Nature
Society Singapore‚Äôs (NSS) SingaporeNature Sightings project proved as much
by roping in residents on the island to upload and share their wildlife
sightings via the&nbsp;<a href="https://tnp.straitstimes.com/news/singapore/residents-became-wildlife-observers-during-circuit-breaker" class="editor-rtfLink" rel="noopener noreferrer nofollow" target="_blank">iNaturalist</a>&nbsp;app.
The app‚Äôs AI capabilities and the society‚Äôs community of experts will then
identify the flora and fauna sighted.</p>
<p>During the circuit breaker from April to June 2020, a total of 3,935 observations
on 987 different species of flora &amp; fauna were submitted, with the
majority of sightings being of plants (30 per cent), birds (30 per cent),
and insects (10 per cent).</p>
<p>The remaining 30 per cent were a mix of fungi, reptiles, mammals, arachnids,
amphibians, and other wildlife.</p>
